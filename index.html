PhD Research - Learning from others: A computational and experimental investigation

Computational models of cognition are useful tools for describing how people integrate information when reasoning, but – despite the fact that humans mainly get their information from one another – these models have until recently been applied to abstract and idealised situations and neglected the role that social inference plays when reasoning in the real world. We know that people update their beliefs differently based on new evidence if that evidence comes from a trusted friend rather than an adversary, but we do not yet have precise accounts for how or why this happens. My PhD work is building on research in A/Prof Perfors' lab exploring how models that incorporate social sampling (i.e., the assumptions people make about the agent providing the information) can account for behaviour in more realistic social contexts.

Supervisors: A/Prof Andrew Perfors, Dr. Keith Ransom

Evidence Accumulation Modelling and Gaze Cueing

Broadly, I’m interested in how evidence accumulation models can be used to strengthen our theoretical understanding of cognitive phenomenon. I’m currently using the Drift Diffusion Model to investigate the cognitive processes underlying the gaze cueing effect (the tendency for people to respond faster to locations that other people are looking).

Collaborators: Kate T. Mckay, Dr. David K. Sewell, Dr. Nathan J. Evans

Multiple Goal Pursuit

I’m interested in how formal theories of dynamic decision-making can help us understand how people balance competing demands. My undergraduate honours thesis investigated how people’s decisions to prioritise different goals change under time-pressure using the multiple goal pursuit model. This thesis was recognised as the best in my honour’s cohort, winning the McElwain Prize.

Supervisor: Dr. Timothy J. Ballard

Perceptions of Replicability in Psychological Science

In this project my collaborators and I designed a survey which was completed by over 500 psychological scientists. We asked them how different attributes of a study (e.g., sample size, subdiscipline, p value, use of open science practices, etc.) influenced their confidence that a study would replicate. See the full published manuscript for more details. Collaborators: Raine Vickers-Jones, Dr. David K. Sewell, Dr. Timothy J. Ballard
